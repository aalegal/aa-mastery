🎯 The Three Mindset Rules

1. Purpose Over People — Lawyer on email ≠ automatic privilege. Ask: What is the PURPOSE of this communication?
2. When in Doubt — Protect & Escalate — 50/50, 60/40, or unsure? Code as Privileged and escalate immediately.
3. Four Corners & Discipline — Decide only from what is written in the document itself.

Attorney–Client Privilege (ACP)

Protects confidential communications between an attorney and client for the purpose of seeking or providing legal advice.

Four Elements (ALL must be true):

  1. Communication — written, oral, or electronic
  2. Between attorney & client (or their agents)
  3. Made in confidence
  4. Primary purpose: legal advice — not just business

✅ Usually ACP:

  • Employee asking counsel: "What is our legal exposure if we do X?"
  • Outside counsel memo analyzing litigation risk to small internal group
  • CEO to outside counsel: "Here are the facts; what are our legal options?"

❌ Usually NOT ACP:

  • Business emails with lawyer copied only as "FYI"
  • Marketing presentations reviewed by legal but containing no legal advice
  • In-house counsel acting as Head of Strategy with no legal content

Work Product Privilege (WPP)

Protects materials prepared in anticipation of litigation or for trial by or for a party or its representatives.

Key Test: "Would this document exist in this form if there were no litigation or investigation?" If NO → likely WPP.

Key Points:

  • Does NOT require an attorney on the document
  • Covers: notes, drafts, chronologies, investigation summaries, strategy docs
  • "Legal asked us to compile this for the DOJ investigation" → WPP signal

Common Interest Privilege (CIP) / Joint Defense

Allows parties with a shared legal interest to share privileged info without waiving attorney-client privilege.

CIP Checklist:

  • Parties share a legal interest (not just commercial)
  • Lawyers are involved in the communication
  • Communication made to advance that legal interest
  • No active waiver — information kept confidential
If you see: "common interest," "joint defense," "JDA" → treat as Privileged and escalate if unclear.

In-House vs Outside Counsel

Core Rule: Privilege follows ROLE, not job title.

Lawyer Hat → ACP:

  • Advising on antitrust risk of a pricing structure
  • Legal analysis of employment discrimination exposure
  • Recommending actions to reduce legal exposure

Business Hat → NOT Privileged:

  • "Our Q4 numbers are weak — let's cut headcount" (no legal content)
  • Meeting invite to weekly sales check-in
  • Holiday party planning with counsel accidentally copied

Four Corners Test

Only use what is written inside the four corners of the document itself.

Do NOT code Responsive just because:

  • The custodian is "usually" responsive
  • The date is within a responsive range
  • The parent or sibling document is responsive

Drafts, Families & Attachments

Drafts & Privilege:

  • Drafts can be privileged — especially with legal comments by counsel
  • Privilege weaker when attorney is doing business/accounting work
  • Drafts intended for public/government filing may lose privilege

Families (Parent + Attachments):

  • If overall family context is privileged → safeguard the whole family unless protocol says otherwise
  • A clearly privileged attachment remains privileged even with a neutral parent

Waiver of Privilege

Privilege is LOST when: client discloses to a non-agent third party · attorney discloses to a third party · email sent with outside third party as recipient · final document filed publicly.
Coder's rule: Do NOT decide waiver yourself. Mark Privileged and Escalate.

🔄 Advanced Review Workflows

✏️ Redaction Workflow — Triggers automatically when you code a document as Redact in the production action field. Two methods:
① Modal Redaction: Full-screen workflow with reason codes (Privilege, PII, Privacy, Relevance), location field, and reviewer note. Add multiple marks per document.
② Drag-to-Redact: Switch to Redaction Mode using the dropdown above the coding panel. Select any text in the document viewer — a popup appears to confirm the reason code and note before applying the mark. Marks appear inline as [REDACTED: Privilege] chips.
📋 Privilege Log (PLOG) Workflow — Triggers automatically after any Withhold or Redact coding where privilege applies. Two methods:
① Auto PLOG Modal: Opens after Save Coding with all metadata pre-filled (Doc ID, From, To, Date, Subject, Custodian, Type). Select ACP / WPP / ACP+WPP / CIP, write your description, and the real-time quality checker flags weak entries.
② Inline PLOG Mode: Switch to PLOG Mode using the dropdown above the coding panel. Fill the quick PLOG form directly in the panel without leaving the document. Quality checker runs live.
Quality Checker Rules: Your PLOG description must be 60+ characters · include privilege indicators (attorney, legal advice, work product, anticipation of litigation) · not reveal the privileged content itself · pass all three checks before saving.
WARNING: Mis-coding a privileged doc as Not Privileged = opposing counsel sees ALL privileged content.

📝 How to Ask a Good Question

  1. Doc ID — exact identifier
  2. Short context — who is emailing whom, about what
  3. Your lean — what you think and why
  4. Why you're unsure — what makes it difficult
"Doc ID: ABC-12345
Sender: in-house counsel; recipients: two managers
Content: Counsel asks managers for facts to assess FTC investigation risk.
I'm leaning: Responsive, ACP + WPP, Highly Confidential.
Question: Is this the right privilege/confidentiality combo for regulator-related internal emails?"

⏱️ Professionalism Reminders

  • Respond to messages within 5 minutes while on shift
  • Report first document coded at start of shift
  • By 1:00 pm NY time — report total documents coded
  • Target speed: Day 1 ~20/hr → Day 2 ~30/hr → Day 3 ~40/hr → Day 4+ 50–60/hr
  • Never sacrifice privilege accuracy for speed

⚫ Privilege Withhold vs. Privilege Redact

🚫

Privilege WITHHOLD

Hide the ENTIRE document. The opposing side gets nothing but a blank placeholder (slipsheet).

When: The entire purpose is legal advice or lawsuit strategy.
Example: CEO to lawyer: "Can we legally fire John?" → WITHHOLD
✏️

Privilege REDACT

Hide only a PART of the document. Black box over the secret sentences, give the rest to opposing side.

When: Mostly business, small piece of legal advice mixed in.
Example: 10-page Minutes. Pages 1–9 business, page 10 has one legal paragraph → REDACT
Simple Rule: If the WHOLE document is a secret → WITHHOLD. If only a SMALL PIECE is a secret → REDACT.

📋 The Privilege Log (PLOG) Guide

The Golden Rule: Write a description that proves a lawyer was involved, but NEVER give away the actual secret.

✅ GOOD PLOG Examples:

✅ "Email reflecting legal advice regarding contract validity."
✅ "Memorandum prepared in anticipation of litigation regarding patent claims."

❌ BAD PLOG Examples:

❌ "Email from lawyer saying we will lose the case because our contract is illegal." → Gives away the secret!
❌ "Email." → Too vague. The judge will reject this.

📊 Pace vs Accuracy — Universal Principles

Speed matters, but only after accuracy is stable. A good reviewer is steady, not rushed.
  • If you start missing obvious things, slow down immediately
  • Long shifts cause mistakes — take short breaks and reset your focus
  • Never sacrifice accuracy just to hit a number
  • A fast wrong review is worse than a slower correct review

📞 Proxy Calls Guide

When should reviewers ask questions to team leads or project managers? And how do you ask in a way that shows understanding, gets a fast useful answer, and builds your professional reputation?

🤔 When Should You Ask?

✅ ESCALATE THESE — Always Ask
🔴 Genuine 50/50 Privilege Gray Area
When: A document has a lawyer's name but the content is ambiguous — could be legal advice or a business decision.
Doc ID [X]. In-house counsel is on this email chain. The subject is "Q4 Strategy" and the content mixes business forecasts with what could be a legal risk analysis on page 3. I'm currently leaning ACP with a note to escalate, but I want to confirm whether the mixed-purpose nature here changes my call or whether I should withhold the entire chain.
Why this works: You've already formed a lean, identified the exact ambiguity, and you're asking a precise question — not asking someone else to do your thinking.
Team LeadPM
🔴 Protocol Conflict — Instructions Don't Match the Document
When: The project protocol says X, but the document is a clear Y. Proceeding either way feels wrong.
The protocol says to code emails to external parties as non-responsive, but this document is addressed to opposing counsel's assistant and contains what looks like a production acknowledgment letter. The protocol doesn't seem to anticipate this type of document. Before I code it non-responsive or escalate further, can you confirm the intent for this category?
Why this works: You spotted a gap in the instructions, described the exact conflict in one sentence, and you're asking for clarification — not a free decision.
Team Lead
🔴 Hot Document
When: You believe a document is substantively important — could directly affect the matter.
I want to flag Doc ID [X] as potentially significant. It's an internal email chain between two executives referencing a meeting with the regulator that isn't mentioned in the protocol's hot custodian list. The content discusses the specific timeline of events the case appears to center on. I've coded it responsive and escalated — just wanted to flag it verbally so you can decide whether it needs QC attention immediately.
Why this works: You've already acted (coded and escalated), you're providing context, not asking someone to review it for you.
Team LeadPM
⛔ DO NOT ESCALATE THESE — Handle Yourself
Does this email need to be coded? (Yes — every document needs a code)
This looks non-responsive, right? (If obvious, trust yourself)
I don't know what ACP means — can you explain? (Use the Reference Guide first)
Should I work faster? (Self-manage your pace)
There's a spelling error in this email — should I flag it? (No)

🧠 Smart Question Formula

Every good question to a team lead or PM has the same four parts.

1
Doc ID / Reference
Always start with the exact document identifier
↓
2
Context in 1–2 sentences
Who is talking to whom? What is the key fact?
↓
3
Your current lean / decision
Tell them what you think the answer is. Showing you've thought about it saves time.
↓
4
The specific question
One precise question they can answer in one sentence.
Never ask a question you could have answered by reading the protocol or this playbook. Every question should demonstrate that you've already done your work.

📋 Smart Questions by Scenario

📧 Email Thread Complexity
What do I do with this email chain?
Doc [X] is a 12-message email chain. The first 8 messages are pure business. Message 9 is from in-house counsel containing what I believe is legal advice. My coding instinct is to code the chain as ACP based on message 9, but I want to confirm — should I break the chain and code the first 8 separately, or keep the family together as privileged?
Team Lead
📎 Attachment Puzzle
Does the attachment follow the email?
Doc [X] is a routine marketing email with a spreadsheet attached. The spreadsheet has a tab labeled "Legal Risk Analysis" that appears to have been prepared by outside counsel. The parent email has no legal content. Protocol says family follows parent, but this attachment seems independently privileged. Should I apply family rule or flag the attachment separately?
Team Lead
⏱️ Pace / Production Check-In
I'm going slow. What should I do?
I want to flag a pace issue proactively. I'm currently averaging 28 docs/hour against a 40/hour target. About 60% of my batch are long email threads with 10+ messages and attachments. I've been reading each fully, which is slowing me. Should I adjust my approach for this batch type, or is the pace understood given the complexity?
Team Lead

Reporting a problem"I've been waiting on this for an hour""Flagging that Doc [X] is pending escalation — wanted to ensure it's in the queue"

🚫 Red Flags — Questions That Signal a Training Gap

Repeatedly asking "Is this privileged?" without a stated lean — shows you aren't applying the four-step test
Asking about the same document type multiple times — signals you haven't internalized the rule
Asking questions directly answered in the protocol — signals you aren't reading project instructions thoroughly
Vague questions with no doc ID — wastes everyone's time and makes you appear disorganized
The best reviewers ask fewer questions — but each one they ask is sharp, precise, and demonstrates serious thinking.

Upon resignation, Joba Company alleges Bukando copied proprietary source code, client data models, and pricing algorithms to a USB drive before his last day — materials covered under a signed NDA and employment agreement. Bukando subsequently joined Rival Analytics Inc., a direct competitor.

Bukando's position (via Kiwingo LLP): The termination was constructive dismissal. He disputes that the materials were trade secrets and claims all work he took was work he personally created and is entitled to retain. He has filed a wrongful termination counterclaim.

Issue Definitions

Any document discussing, referencing, or evidencing the NDA signed by Bukando, violations thereof, or awareness of confidentiality obligations.

Issue 2: Trade Secret Theft

Any document relating to proprietary code, algorithms, data models, pricing structures, client lists, or evidence of unauthorized copying or transfer of Joba Company assets.

Issue 3: Competitive Damage

Any document showing Bukando's use of Joba's proprietary materials at Rival Analytics, or demonstrating competitive harm to Joba Company.

Issue 4: Wrongful Termination (Counter)

Any document relating to Bukando's employment conditions, constructive dismissal allegations, performance reviews, or HR communications.
