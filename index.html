🎯 The Three Mindset Rules

1. Purpose Over People — Lawyer on email ≠ automatic privilege. Ask: What is the PURPOSE of this communication?
2. When in Doubt — Protect & Escalate — 50/50, 60/40, or unsure? Code as Privileged and escalate immediately.
3. Four Corners & Discipline — Decide only from what is written in the document itself. Not custodian, date range, or family.

Attorney–Client Privilege (ACP)

Protects confidential communications between an attorney and client for the purpose of seeking or providing legal advice.

Four Elements (ALL must be true):

  1. Communication — written, oral, or electronic
  2. Between attorney & client (or their agents)
  3. Made in confidence
  4. Primary purpose: legal advice — not just business

✅ Usually ACP:

  • Employee asking counsel: "What is our legal exposure if we do X?"
  • Outside counsel memo analyzing litigation risk to small internal group
  • CEO to outside counsel: "Here are the facts; what are our legal options?"

❌ Usually NOT ACP:

  • Business emails with lawyer copied only as "FYI"
  • Marketing presentations reviewed by legal but containing no legal advice
  • In-house counsel acting as Head of Strategy with no legal content

Work Product Privilege (WPP)

Protects materials prepared in anticipation of litigation or for trial by or for a party or its representatives.

Key Test: "Would this document exist in this form if there were no litigation or investigation?" If NO → likely WPP.

Key Points:

  • Does NOT require an attorney on the document
  • Covers: notes, drafts, chronologies, investigation summaries, strategy docs
  • "Legal asked us to compile this for the DOJ investigation" → WPP signal

✅ Usually WPP:

  • Investigation report at explicit request of litigation counsel
  • Timeline compiled because legal asked for DOJ prep
  • Deposition Q&A drafted by outside counsel
  • Attorney's margin notes on a non-privileged contract

❌ Usually NOT WPP:

  • Monthly sales dashboard generated as routine business for years
  • Engineering report written before any lawsuit or legal risk was mentioned

Common Interest Privilege (CIP) / Joint Defense

Allows parties with a shared legal interest to share privileged info without waiving attorney-client privilege.

CIP Checklist:

  • Parties share a legal interest (not just commercial)
  • Lawyers are involved in the communication
  • Communication made to advance that legal interest
  • No active waiver — information kept confidential
If you see: "common interest," "joint defense," "JDA" → treat as Privileged and escalate if unclear.

✅ Usually CIP:

  • Co-defendants' counsel sharing strategy under a signed JDA
  • Company + executive (separate counsel) sharing privileged DOJ analysis

❌ NOT CIP:

  • Business teams emailing financial models (no lawyers, no legal strategy)
  • Two friends sharing documents with no lawyers involved

In-House vs Outside Counsel

Core Rule: Privilege follows ROLE, not job title.

Lawyer Hat → ACP:

  • Advising on antitrust risk of a pricing structure
  • Legal analysis of employment discrimination exposure
  • Recommending actions to reduce legal exposure

Business Hat → NOT Privileged:

  • "Our Q4 numbers are weak — let's cut headcount" (no legal content)
  • Meeting invite to weekly sales check-in
  • Holiday party planning with counsel accidentally copied
  • Email signed as "VP of Operations" with operational content only

Common Pitfalls:

  • "CC'ing Legal so they're in the loop" with no legal advice → NOT privileged
  • "Legal said we're fine" (mentioned but not analyzed) → NOT privileged
  • Mixed email (legal primary + business secondary) → ACP, primary purpose controls

Four Corners Test

Only use what is written inside the four corners of the document itself.

Do NOT code Responsive just because:

  • The custodian is "usually" responsive
  • The date is within a responsive range
  • The parent or sibling document is responsive

DO ask:

"Is the information that makes this responsive contained within the four corners of this document?"

Drafts, Families & Attachments

Drafts & Privilege:

  • Drafts can be privileged — especially with legal comments or analysis by counsel
  • Privilege weaker when attorney is doing business/accounting work
  • Drafts intended for public/government filing may lose privilege

Families (Parent + Attachments):

  • If overall family context is privileged → safeguard the whole family unless protocol says otherwise
  • A clearly privileged attachment remains privileged even with a neutral parent
  • Factual spreadsheet in privileged family + protocol silent → Privileged + Escalate

Waiver of Privilege

Privilege is LOST when: client discloses to a non-agent third party · attorney discloses to a third party · email sent with outside third party as recipient · final document filed publicly.

Tricky Scenarios → Always Escalate:

  • CEO forwards legal advice email to CFO with business commentary
  • Executive forwards counsel's privileged email to third-party consultant
  • Legal analysis shared with outside auditor
Coder's rule: Do NOT decide waiver yourself. Mark Privileged and Escalate.

📝 How to Ask a Good Question

Always include:

  1. Doc ID — exact identifier
  2. Short context — who is emailing whom, about what
  3. Your lean — what you think and why
  4. Why you're unsure — what makes it difficult
"Doc ID: ABC-12345
Sender: in-house counsel; recipients: two managers
Content: Counsel asks managers for facts to assess FTC investigation risk.
I'm leaning: Responsive, ACP + WPP, Highly Confidential.
Question: Is this the right privilege/confidentiality combo for regulator-related internal emails?"

⏱️ Professionalism Reminders

  • Report first document coded at start of shift
  • By 1:00 pm NY time — report total documents coded
  • At sign-out — report final count
  • Target speed: Day 1 ~20/hr → Day 2 ~30/hr → Day 3 ~40/hr → Day 4+ 50–60/hr
  • Never sacrifice privilege accuracy for speed

⚫ Privilege Withhold vs. Privilege Redact

Once you find a legal secret, you must decide how to hide it. You have exactly two choices.

🚫

Privilege WITHHOLD

Hide the ENTIRE document. The opposing side gets nothing but a blank placeholder (slipsheet).

When to use: The entire purpose of the document is legal advice or lawsuit strategy. There is nothing shareable.
Example: Email from CEO to lawyer: "Can we legally fire John?" — The whole email is about legal advice. Code as: WITHHOLD
✏️

Privilege REDACT

Hide only a PART of the document. Draw a digital black box over the secret sentences, give the rest to the opposing side.

When to use: The document is mostly normal business, but a small piece of legal advice is mixed in.
Example: 10-page Weekly Meeting Minutes. Pages 1–9 are sales goals. Page 10 has one legal advice paragraph. Black-box that paragraph only. Code as: REDACT
Simple Rule: If the WHOLE document is a secret → WITHHOLD. If only a SMALL PIECE is a secret → REDACT.

🔀 The Decision Flowchart

Follow this exact path for every single document. Do not skip steps!

1
Are there lawyers involved?
Look at sender, receivers, and the text. YES → Go to Step 3. NO → Go to Step 2.
↓
2
Is it Work Product?
Was this document made specifically to prepare for a lawsuit? YES → Code as Work Product. NO → NOT PRIVILEGED. Stop.
↓
3
Is it Legal Advice?
Is the main topic asking for or giving legal advice (not business advice)? YES → Go to Step 4. NO → NOT PRIVILEGED. Stop.
↓
4
Is it completely private?
Are outside strangers (vendors, reporters) on the email? YES (strangers present) → Privilege BROKEN. NOT PRIVILEGED. NO (purely internal) → Go to Step 5.
↓
5
WITHHOLD or REDACT?
Is the entire document about legal advice? → WITHHOLD
Is it mostly business with a little legal advice inside? → REDACT

📋 The Privilege Log (PLOG) Guide

When you hide a document (withhold or redact), you must put it on a list called the Privilege Log (PLOG). This list is given to the opposing side so they can see WHAT you're hiding — but not WHY in detail.

The Golden Rule: Write a description that proves a lawyer was involved, but NEVER give away the actual secret.

❌ BAD PLOG Examples:

❌ "Email from lawyer saying we will lose the case because our contract is illegal."
→ You just gave away the secret! The opposing side now knows your weakness.
❌ "Email."
→ Too vague. The judge will reject this and may order you to produce the document.

✅ GOOD PLOG Examples:

✅ "Email reflecting legal advice regarding contract validity."
→ States the topic without giving the answer. Perfect.
✅ "Memorandum prepared in anticipation of litigation regarding patent claims."
→ Identifies it as Work Product without revealing the strategy.

📝 Copy-Paste PLOG Templates:

FOR ACP:
[Document Type] reflecting legal advice regarding [General Topic].
Example: Email reflecting legal advice regarding employee termination.
FOR WORK PRODUCT:
[Document Type] prepared in anticipation of litigation regarding [General Topic].
Example: Memorandum prepared in anticipation of litigation regarding patent claims.
FOR REDACTION:
[Document Type] containing redacted legal advice regarding [General Topic].
Example: Meeting minutes containing redacted legal advice regarding regulatory compliance.

🚩 Keywords & Red Flags

Look out for these words. They often (but not always) mean a document is privileged.

✅ Strong Legal Keywords

  • Legal advice / Legal strategy
  • Counsel / General Counsel / Outside Counsel
  • Lawsuit / Litigation / Deposition
  • Draft for legal review
  • "Seeking your legal guidance..."
  • In anticipation of litigation
  • Attorney work product

⚠️ Dangerous Misleading Phrases

"Privileged and Confidential"
Every lawyer puts this at the bottom of their emails automatically. It does NOT magically make a lunch invitation privileged! Read the actual message.
"For your review"
This might just mean reviewing for typos. It must be for LEGAL review to be privileged.
"Priv" label on a business email
An executive typing "Priv" on a sales strategy email does not make it privileged. Apply the four-step test.

✅ The Ultimate Reviewer Checklist

Check these boxes before you hit "Save" on any privileged document.

☐Did I check the project's specific protocol/instructions first? (Protocol always overrides this playbook)
☐Is there a lawyer involved? (Sender, recipient, or mentioned in the text)
☐If there is no lawyer, is this clearly lawsuit preparation (Work Product)?
☐Did I verify this is Legal Advice and not just business or social chat?
☐Is the communication completely private? (No outside third parties on the email)
☐Did I choose the correct action? (WITHHOLD the whole document OR REDACT just a part)
☐Did I write a safe PLOG description that protects the secret without giving it away?
☐Did I check the attachments to ensure the whole "Family" is coded correctly?
REMEMBER: Whenever in doubt, ALWAYS ask your supervisor for help.
